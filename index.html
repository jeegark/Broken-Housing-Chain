A brownfield viability story · London

The Broken Chain

A half-acre car workshop in south-east London could become 35 new homes. The maths says it won't. Here's exactly where the chain snaps — link by link.

Scroll to begin
The site

Meet Meridian Autos.

It's a working car-repair garage on half an acre, eight minutes' walk from a Zone 2/3 station. As a running business it's worth about £1 million. On paper, it's a planner's dream: exactly the kind of brownfield plot the London Plan wants turned into housing.

A developer draws up a scheme: 35 flats, four to six storeys, averaging 720 sq ft each. Build cost runs to about £8.9 million once you add £500k of "abnormals" — the cleanup, contamination and groundwork that come free with any old industrial site.

So far, so good. Now watch the chain break in three places.

1 The first break

The land won't trade.

Before a single brick is laid, the developer has to buy the site. And the price the rulebook allows is not the price the owners can afford to accept.

The rulebook's number

Planning uses a formula called Existing Use Value plus a premium. Take what the site is worth today (£1m), add a 15% sweetener, and you get the most the developer is "allowed" to pay.

GLA benchmark land value: £1.15m

The owners' real number

But David and Sarah, who own the garage, don't see a formula — they see a tax bill and a life change. Capital gains tax takes ~£160k. Professional fees take £30k. Closing or moving the business costs £100–150k.

To walk away whole, they need £1.29m–£1.34m

So they say no — and here's why

Sell at the rulebook's £1.15m and the taxman, the professional fees and the cost of shutting up shop swallow roughly a third of it. David and Sarah would be left with about £0.8m in hand — less than the £1m their business is already worth to them as a running garage, and they'd have given up their livelihood on top.

Why give up something worth £1m to walk away with less? They don't. The "brownfield opportunity" stays a car workshop, and nothing is built at all.

2 The second break

The numbers don't add up.

Suppose the owners give in and sell at the rulebook price of £1.15m. Now the developer has to make the scheme pay for itself. Watch the money pour in — and drain away.

The money coming in

Sell all 35 flats — 28 at full market price, 7 as "affordable" at a discount — and the scheme raises about £15 million. This is the whole pot. But the mix matters: the 28 market flats bring in the lion's share, while the 7 affordable flats sell for a fraction of that.

Building it eats most of it

Construction and the brownfield cleanup take ~£8.9m straight off the top — nearly 60% of the pot gone before anything else is paid.

Remaining: ~£6.0m

Then the rest of the costs

Borrowing the money costs ~£0.76m. Architects, engineers and consultants take ~£1.07m. Council levies — CIL and Section 106 — take £0.5m. Selling the flats takes another ~£0.4m.

Total costs so far: ~£11.7m

A margin for what could go wrong

No bank funds a build with no safety net. Roughly £2.6m — about 17.5% — has to be set aside as a margin for risk: the cushion that absorbs the hit if build costs overrun, materials jump, the job runs late, or the flats sell for less than hoped. Without it, one bad surprise turns the whole scheme into a loss.

Margin for risk set aside: ~£2.6m

What's left for the land

After everything, the cash left to actually buy the site is just ~£0.68m. But the land was supposed to cost £1.15m.

Shortfall: ~£0.47m below what the land costs

And that's the generous version

This already assumes only 20% affordable housing — an emergency setting. Apply London's standard 35% target and the scheme is "structurally unworkable."

3 The third break · affordability

The developer can't sell all the flats.

Imagine the first two breaks are somehow fixed and the flats actually get built. There's one problem left, and it's an affordability break — the one no viability spreadsheet shows: with the props that made buying affordable now gone, who can still afford to buy them?

The exit nobody models

A viability appraisal assumes every flat sells, on time, at the price on the sheet. Reality is a shrinking crowd of people who can afford a new-build flat in inner London.

The demand props get pulled away

Help to Buy — which underwrote a huge share of new-build sales — ended in 2023 with nothing in its place. Stamp duty, high mortgage rates and capped first-time-buyer relief each chip away at the crowd.

The pool of likely buyers shrinks

Each pressure removes more buyers who could have stretched to a £480k flat. The crowd that's left is far smaller — and far less certain.

And that loops straight back to the land

Here's the knock-on effect. The less certain a developer is that the flats will actually sell, the bigger the safety cushion they need before they'll risk building at all — the same way you'd demand a steeper discount before taking a real gamble.

But that cushion comes out of the same pot. A bigger cushion means even less money left over to pay for the land.

So the price they can afford to offer for the site drops even lower — making Problem 1 worse, not better.

Three breaks, one chain.

Each link fails on its own — and fixing just one doesn't restart the others.

1

The land won't trade

The benchmark price ignores tax, business value and moving costs — so owners never sell, and the site never starts.

2

The numbers don't add up

Even at the rulebook land price, construction, finance, fees, levies and a margin for risk leave too little to buy the site — and any real affordable-housing requirement makes it worse.

3

The developer can't sell all the flats

With the props for first-time buyers gone, selling the finished flats is uncertain — so developers need a bigger safety cushion, which leaves even less to pay for the land, widening the very gap that started the problem.

The takeaway

Pull one lever and nothing moves.

"These all need to be in lockstep to make the system work again."

Land pricing, building costs and buyer demand are not three separate problems. They're three links in one chain — and a chain only works when every link holds. That's why even free land wouldn't, on its own, get these 35 homes built.
